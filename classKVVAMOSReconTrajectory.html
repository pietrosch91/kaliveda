KVVAMOSReconTrajectory

Handle data used to reconstruct the trajectory of a nucleus in VAMOS

This class allows to handle data or coordinates used in the reconstruction of the trajectory in the VAMOS spectrometer. This class is used both for the reconstruction from the focal plane to the target point and for the inverse reconstruction by the friend classes KVVAMOSReconNuc and KVVAMOSTransferMatrix. <figure>


<figcaption>Fig1. - Representation of the trajectory at the focal plane and at the target point (lab frame)
            within different coordinate systems. The angle alpha is the rotation angle of VAMOS
            around the Y-axis in the VAMOS coordinate system. </figcaption> </figure>

The nucleus trajectory to be reconstructed is represented in Fig1. by the velocity vector:

Begin_Latex( fontsize=20 ) #vec{v} #(){v_{x},v_{y},v_{z} } End_Latex

Coordinate systems

1) In laboratory frame of reference (lab-frame)

By convention, the direct coordinate system in KaliVeda (called INDRA coord. syst. in Fig1.) has the target position for its center. The beam direction defines the positive (+ve) Z-axis, with respect to which polar angles are measured. The +ve X-axis is taken to be vertical, and azimuth angles increase when going clockwise from the +ve X-axis, looking towards the +ve z direction. The angles in the INDRA spherical coord. syst. are named:

Begin_Latex( fontsize=20 ) #theta (polar angle) #phi (azimuth angle) End_Latex

theta varies between 0 and 180 degrees while phi varies between 0 and 359.999... degrees.

In the VAMOS community, the convention is a little bit different. The direct coord. syst. (calle VAMOS coord. syst. in Fig1.) is also centered at the target point but the +ve X-axis is taken to be horizontal and the +ve Y-axis is taken to be vertical. The +ve Z-axis, polar angles and azimuth angles are defined as previously. The angles in the VAMOS spherical coord. system are named:

Begin_Latex( fontsize=20 ) #theta_{l} (polar angle) #phi_{l} (azimuth angle) End_Latex

the polar angle varies between 0 to 180 degrees and the azimuth angle between -179.999... to 180 degrees. They are accessible by calling the methods GetThetaL and GetPhiL

For the trajectory reconstruction (see KVVAMOSTransferMatrix) , the algorithm does not used the polar coordinates but other coordinates of the standard ion optics formalism. In this formalism two angles are defined:

  • theta_v: angle between the Z-axis and the projection of the velocity vector of the trajectory on the XZ plane (symmetry plane) in the lab-frame of reference. It is returned by the method GetThetaV;
  • phi_v: angle between the velocity vector of the trajectory and its projection on the XZ plane (symmetry plane) in the lab-frame. It is returned by the method GetPhiV.

Both angles vary between -90 to 90 degrees. The relations between the cartesian coordinates of the velocity and these angles in the lab-frame are:

Begin_Latex( fontsize=20 ) v_{x} = v sin(#theta_{v}) cos(#phi_{v}) v_{y} = v sin(#phi_{v}) v_{z} = v cos(#theta_{v}) cos(#phi_{v}) End_Latex Begin_Latex( fontsize=20 ) #theta_{v} = arctan( v_{x}/v_{z} ) #phi_{v} = arcsin( v_{y}/v ) End_Latex

2) In Focal-Plane frame of reference (FP-frame)

The origin of the direct coord. syst. is the center of the focal plane, the +ve X-axis is taken to be horizontal and the +ve Y-axis is taken to be vertical as for the VAMOS coord. syst. in the lab-frame. The +ve Z-axis follows the reference trajectory, polar angles and azimuth angles are defined as previously.

The relations between the cartesian coordinates of the velocity and the different angles used in the FP-frame of reference are:

Begin_Latex( fontsize=20 ) v_{x} = v sin(#theta_{f}) cos(#phi_{f}) v_{y} = v sin(#phi_{f}) v_{z} = v cos(#theta_{f}) cos(#phi_{f}) End_Latex Begin_Latex( fontsize=20 ) #theta_{f} = arctan( v_{x}/v_{z} ) #phi_{f} = arcsin( v_{y}/v ) End_Latex

These two angles vary between -90 to 90 degrees and are accessible by calling the methods GetThetaF and GetPhiF.

The intersection point of the trajectory and the focal plane is located by the cartesian coordinates Xf and Yf (see Fig1.) in cm.
